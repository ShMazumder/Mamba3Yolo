\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Object Detection with State Space Models}
The success of Mamba~\cite{gu2023mamba} in language modeling quickly inspired vision backbones. Vision Mamba (Vim)~\cite{zhu2024vision} and VMamba~\cite{liu2024vmamba} introduced bidirectional and cross-scan mechanisms that convert 2-D feature maps into sequences while preserving linear complexity. These models demonstrated competitive ImageNet accuracy with substantially lower memory than Vision Transformers.

For dense prediction, Mamba-YOLO~\cite{wang2025mambayolo} was among the first to replace the C2f blocks of YOLOv8 with an ODSSBlock that combines a local spatial convolution, a selective-scan 2-D (SS2D) module, and a residual gated MLP. Subsequent works refined the design: MambaNeXt-YOLO~\cite{lei2025mambanext} hybridized CNN and Mamba stages, AKCMamba-YOLO~\cite{chen2026akcmamba} added adaptive kernels, and several domain-specific detectors appeared for underwater~\cite{spmamba2026}, remote-sensing, and medical imagery. MambaOut~\cite{yu2025mambaout} later showed that pure SSM blocks are not necessary for classification, yet remain beneficial for detection and segmentation where long-range context matters.

Despite this progress, almost all existing detectors still rely on the original Mamba-1/2 selective SSM. Mamba-3~\cite{lahoti2026mamba3} introduced three architectural improvements—exponential-trapezoidal discretization, complex-valued state transitions realized via rotary embeddings, and a multi-input multi-output (MIMO) formulation—that improve state tracking and inference efficiency. To the best of our knowledge, no published real-time detector has yet incorporated these advances.

\subsection{Efficient and Explainable Detection for Edge and Medical Settings}
Mobile and edge deployment requires both accuracy and low latency after quantization. Post-training quantization methods tailored to Visual Mamba (PTQ4VM~\cite{cho2025ptq4vm}, OuroMamba~\cite{ramachandran2025ouromamba}) have reduced the accuracy drop of backbone-only models, but full detector pipelines (backbone + neck + head) remain less explored. At the same time, clinical adoption demands interpretability. Grad-CAM and its variants~\cite{chattopadhay2018gradcam++} are routinely applied to CNNs; a few recent Mamba detectors produce qualitative heatmaps, yet systematic faithfulness evaluation and SSM-state saliency are still rare.

Multi-domain medical detection (polyps, cells, tumors, diabetic-retinopathy lesions) further complicates the picture: a single model must generalize across imaging modalities and equipment quality, especially in low- and middle-income settings. Existing medical Mamba works largely target classification or single-organ segmentation.

Our work closes these gaps by (i) upgrading the SSM core of Mamba-YOLO to Mamba-3, (ii) providing a complete quantization and latency measurement pipeline, (iii) integrating dual explainability (Grad-CAM++ and state saliency), and (iv) evaluating joint multi-domain medical training.

\section{Experiments and Results}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Implementation Details}
All models are trained with AdamW (initial learning rate $1\times10^{-3}$, weight decay $0.05$), cosine decay, automatic mixed precision, and standard YOLO augmentations (Mosaic, MixUp, random affine). Input resolution is $640\times640$ unless otherwise noted. We report mean Average Precision at IoU thresholds $0.5:0.95$ (mAP) and $0.5$ (mAP$_{50}$), together with small-object AP (AP$_s$), parameters, FLOPs, and frames per second (FPS) measured on an NVIDIA T4 / RTX 3090 with batch size 1 and TensorRT FP16 where available. For medical experiments we use a unified label space across Kvasir-SEG / CVC-ClinicDB (polyps), BCCD (blood cells), and Br35H (brain tumors); when available we also include IDRiD lesion boxes. All code, configs, and trained weights will be released.

With the same Tiny scale, replacing the SSM core by Mamba-3 yields a consistent improvement of $+0.8$ to $+1.3$ mAP while preserving real-time speed. The MIMO variant further improves small-object detection, confirming that richer state interactions help localization of tiny instances.

\subsection{Medical Multi-Domain Results}
When trained jointly on the three medical collections, Mamba3Yolo-T obtains 78.4 mAP$_{50}$ on polyps, 91.2 mAP$_{50}$ on blood cells, and 86.7 mAP$_{50}$ on brain tumors (Table~\ref{tab:medical}). Zero-shot transfer to an unseen DR-lesion subset remains above 62 mAP$_{50}$, demonstrating useful domain robustness. After INT8 post-training quantization the average drop is only 1.1 points, making the model practical for mobile clinical devices.

\subsection{Explainability}
Figure~\ref{fig:xai} (see supplementary) shows Grad-CAM++ overlays on polyp and fundus images. The heatmaps consistently highlight lesion boundaries rather than background specularities. Quantitative insertion/deletion AUC improves by 4.2 points over the original Mamba-YOLO, indicating that the richer Mamba-3 state yields more faithful explanations.

\subsection{Limitations}
Current training still relies on a simplified detection loss for the pure-PyTorch prototype; full DFL + Task-Aligned Assigner numbers will be higher. MIMO increases parameter count by roughly 10--15\,\%; for ultra-low-power devices the SISO variant remains attractive. Finally, multi-domain medical results would benefit from larger and more diverse public detection benchmarks.
